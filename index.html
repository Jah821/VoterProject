Building the Political Power of People of Color

We are dedicated to building the political power of marginalized people of color.

We created this website in hopes to increase minority voter turnout numbers for the upcoming 2021 New Jersey General Election. Through the creation of this website we hope to inform all voters on the candidates that they are selecting to represent them and their communities. We also want to make it as easy as possible to sift through the vast ammounts of voting information and show voters that this year they have options on how and when to vote.

How to Vote

It’s always an election year. Even when there aren’t national or statewide elections taking place, local elections are happening across the country. These elections determine local leaders and make decisions on issues that impact us close to home – from school board members to funding for law enforcement to mask mandates. Check out our pages to find all the information you need to get out and vote in your area!

Black Americans showed the sharpest decline in voter turnout– 7.1 percent since 2012. At 59.6 percent, it was the lowest black turnout rate since 2000.

Only after the Civil War, with the passage of the 15th Amendment in 1870, were Black men given the right to vote. Not until the 19th Amendment passed in 1920 were Black women allowed to vote. Even then, a vast system of obstacles — including poll taxes and literacy tests — prevented many Black people from voting.

Election officials typically remove the names of people who have died or moved from rosters of registered voters. But some states go further, removing names of people who have not voted in recent elections — a practice that is prone to error and may be unconstitutional.

Under Florida law, nearly a quarter of Black adults are barred from voting because of past felony convictions. In 2018, Florida voters approved, by a large majority, a measure allowing some 1.4 million felons to vote. Republican lawmakers, backed by court decisions written by Republican-appointed judges, have largely blocked the Florida measure from going into effect.
